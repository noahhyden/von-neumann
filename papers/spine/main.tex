\begin{abstract}
A programme of self-replicating space manufacturing is usually modelled one scale at a time: a single factory that copies itself, a local fleet that disperses across a solar system, or a settlement front that crosses a galaxy. We thread one sourced factory through all three folds, so that a quantity every scale needs, the time to build one copy, is derived once rather than assumed three times over. The result is a clean cross-scale statement. The copy time that sets a local fleet's doubling clock, about 582 days for a lunar-regolith seed, is the same quantity that, at galactic scale, is under one part in a million of the roughly two-million-year time a powered front takes to fill a stellar field. The constraint that governs one scale is a rounding error at the next. Because that copy time is a lunar-factory stand-in for an interstellar probe whose own bill of materials is not yet known, we do not defend its exact value; we show the conclusion survives not knowing it. Across a copy-time sweep of five orders of magnitude the manufacturing dwell stays below one percent of the fill until the copy time is about fifteen thousand times its nominal value, so an error of four orders of magnitude in the build cadence does not change the verdict. Measured directly, seed by seed, the dwell costs a median of about a third of a percent of exploration time for the fastest slingshot policy and is within seed-to-seed noise for the slower one. The picture is therefore robust: manufacturing time is the binding clock for a local fleet, where transit is days, and a negligible tax on interstellar exploration, where transit is millennia.
\end{abstract}
