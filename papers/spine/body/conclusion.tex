\section{Conclusion}

Threading one sourced factory through the single-factory, local-fleet, and galactic-front
models lets a single quantity, the time to build one copy, be derived where the physics fixes
it rather than assumed separately at each scale. Doing so answers a cross-scale question no one
model can: the manufacturing cadence that is the entire clock for a local fleet, about 582 days
for a lunar-regolith seed, is under one part in a million of a powered galactic fill, and the
directly measured dwell tax on a fast slingshot front is a median of about a third of a percent
of exploration time. Because the copy time is a proxy for a probe whose bill of materials is
not yet known, the central result is stated as a robustness margin rather than a point value:
the verdict holds for any copy time within four orders of magnitude of the proxy. Manufacturing
time is thus the binding constraint on self-replication in a solar system and a rounding error
on the way across a galaxy, where transit dominates. The natural next step is to replace the
lunar-factory proxy with a sourced interstellar-probe bill of materials, which would turn the
numerator from an estimate into a measurement without, on present evidence, moving the
conclusion.
