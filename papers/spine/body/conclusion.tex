\section{Conclusion}

Threading one sourced factory through the single-factory, local-fleet, and galactic-front
models lets a single quantity, the time to build one copy, be derived where the physics fixes
it rather than assumed separately at each scale. Doing so answers a cross-scale question no one
model can: the manufacturing cadence that is the entire clock for a local fleet, about 582 days
for a lunar-regolith seed, lengthens a powered galactic fill by only about one part in fifty
thousand (the per-copy ratio is smaller still, under one part in a million), and the directly
measured dwell tax on the fastest slingshot front is a median of about a third of a percent of
exploration time. Because the copy time is a proxy for a probe whose bill of materials is not
yet known, the central result is stated as a robustness margin rather than a point value, and
the margin is bounded honestly: for the powered galactic fill the verdict holds for any copy
time within about two orders of magnitude of the proxy, though for the fastest fronts the
headroom is only a few-fold. Manufacturing time is thus the binding constraint on
self-replication in a solar system and, for a powered cruise, a rounding error on the way across
a galaxy, where transit dominates; it re-enters the ledger only as the front is driven to
slingshot or directed-energy speeds. The natural next step is to replace the lunar-factory proxy
with a sourced interstellar-probe bill of materials, which would turn the numerator from an
estimate into a measurement without, on present evidence, moving the powered galactic
conclusion.
