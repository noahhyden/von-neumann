\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The headline: manufacturing is a rounding error at galactic scale}
For the sourced lunar-regolith seed, \eqref{eq:copy} gives a copy time of about 582~days,
so the derived dwell of \eqref{eq:dwell} is $\tau \approx 1.59$~years. At fleet scale this
same 582~days is the doubling clock: neighbours are days apart, so build time is the only
clock that matters. Run as the settlement-front dwell for a powered cruise across a field of
1200 Sun-like stars, it sits against a fill time $T_{100} \approx 1.9\times10^{6}$~years,
reproducing to the same order of magnitude the multi-million-year powered timescales of the
model we build on~\citep{nicholson-forgan-2013}.

Two numbers describe how little this dwell matters, and it is essential to keep them apart. The
per-copy ratio of \eqref{eq:frac} is
\begin{equation}
f \;\approx\; 8.5\times10^{-7},
\end{equation}
one build cadence set against the whole galactic fill: under one part in a million. But the
front pays one dwell at \emph{every} settlement, so the physically meaningful quantity is the
cumulative slowdown of the fill when the derived dwell is switched on against the old zero-dwell
baseline. On the same field that cumulative tax is
\begin{equation}
\Delta T_{100}/T_{100} \;\approx\; 1.5\times10^{-5},
\label{eq:cumtax}
\end{equation}
under one part in fifty thousand: about eighteen times $f$, which is just the number of
settlements on the critical path from the seed to the last star, and still utterly negligible.
It is this cumulative tax, not $f$, that measures manufacturing's share of the timeline, and it
is the one we hold to the one-percent bar below; leading with $f$ alone would overstate the
margin by that critical-path factor.

\subsection{The result survives not knowing the copy time}
The copy time is a lunar-factory proxy, so the case cannot rest on its exact value; it rests on
the margin. Fig.~\ref{fig:margin} sweeps the copy time across five orders of magnitude and
records both measures of the dwell's cost. The per-copy ratio $f$ rises in step with the copy
time and would not reach one percent until the copy time was about $1.5\times10^{4}$ times
nominal. The cumulative tax rises the same way but from an eighteen-fold higher floor, so it
reaches the one-percent bar sooner: it is still only about 0.15~percent at a hundredfold copy
time and does not cross one percent until a few hundredfold, a copy time of a few centuries
rather than a year and a half. In round terms the verdict survives a two-order-of-magnitude
error in the build cadence, not the four orders the per-copy ratio alone would suggest. Either
way the conclusion is insensitive to the one number we cannot yet source, which is the strongest
statement we can make given the gap. And because the copy time is machinery-limited at 1~AU
(Section~\ref{sec:model}), the documented uncertainty in the solar-array inputs does not move it
at all; the load-bearing input is the machinery build rate, whose plausible variation is a small
sub-interval of this margin.

\subsection{The margin shrinks as the front is driven faster}
The dwell's share of the fill is largest for the fastest front, because a fixed dwell is a
larger slice of a shorter fill. Table~\ref{tab:policy} sweeps the three named policies on a
common 400-star field and reports, for each, the cumulative tax at the nominal copy time and the
copy-time multiplier at which that tax would reach one percent. The trend is monotone and is the
honest boundary of the claim: for a powered cruise the copy time could grow about six-hundredfold
before manufacturing cost one percent of the fill, but for nearest-target slingshots, whose fill
is two hundred times shorter, the dwell already costs about a third of a percent (next
subsection) and only a threefold longer copy time would reach one percent. The directed-energy
cruise at 0.1 to 0.2~$c$ that beamed-propulsion concepts
target~\citep{lubin-2016,armstrong-sandberg-2013} lies beyond the slingshot regime we simulate;
it would shorten the fill further and shrink the margin further, so the crossover is nearest for
exactly the fastest missions.

\subsection{Measuring the tax directly}
The cumulative tax quoted above is a single deterministic field; to give it a spread we measure
it over a 24-seed ensemble on the fast slingshot policies, where it is largest
(Fig.~\ref{fig:tax}). For a multi-million-year powered fill the same difference is far too small
to resolve by simulation, which is itself the finding, so the ensemble covers the slingshots
only. For nearest-target slingshots the tax is tight and clearly resolved at a median of
0.32~percent of exploration time (interquartile range 0.26 to 0.36~percent). For max-boost
slingshots the per-seed values scatter from about $-6$ to $+10$~percent around a median near one
percent: at this field size the dwell's effect is within the seed-to-seed noise of the fill
time, so we report it as positive-but-unresolved rather than as a point estimate. Reporting a
single seed here, as an earlier draft of the underlying finding did, would have hidden that
spread.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.66\linewidth]{fig_dwell_tax.pdf}
\caption{The measured dwell tax (fractional increase in fill time from switching the derived
dwell on) per seed for the two slingshot policies, 24 seeds each, event-resolved on a 400-star
field. Bars are the ensemble median, shaded bands the interquartile range. Nearest-target
slingshots give a tight, resolved 0.32~percent; max-boost scatters across zero and is within
seed noise.}
\label{fig:tax}
\end{figure}

Two checks confirm the measurement is not an artifact. Halving the integration timestep leaves
the nearest-slingshot tax stable and converging on the event-resolved value, whereas a timestep
coarse against the dwell under-resolves it; this is the same discretization hazard identified for
the underlying front model, so we report event-resolved values throughout. Varying the field
size over 200, 400, and 800 stars moves the median tax only between 0.31 and 0.34~percent, a mild
monotone increase, so the small-field measurement is representative over the range tested.
