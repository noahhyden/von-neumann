\section{Results: the coordination tax}
\label{sec:results}

We measured the cost as a paired ensemble: 32 seeded galaxies, each filled twice on the
\emph{identical} field, once with perfect information and once with the light-speed gate of
(\ref{eq:gate}), all at the resolved event-driven timestep. Two quantities matter: the relative
slowdown in the time to fill the field, and the number of wasted journeys, trips to stars a
probe finds already settled on arrival. Reporting the distribution across the ensemble follows
the seeded Monte Carlo practice of the field \cite{forgan-2009}; we give the median with a
seeded percentile-bootstrap 95\% confidence interval and a two-sided sign test over the paired
seeds.

\subsection{There is no fill-time tax; the coarse-timestep one is an artifact}
Light-speed lag does not measurably slow the fill of the field. Fig.~\ref{fig:dt} shows why the
literature intuition says otherwise. Run with the source model's fixed timestep the fill-100\%
penalty for nearest-star slingshot flight is a median of 30.3\%, positive in all 32 seeds -- a
large, robust-looking tax. It is an artifact of the step. As the timestep is refined the penalty
falls monotonically -- 27.6\%, 25.0\%, 12.8\%, 5.3\% at 2000, 1000, 500, 250 years -- and at the
event-driven limit it is a median of 0.0\%, no longer distinguishable from zero. The coarse step
had batched many launches into one window so they decided from the same stale snapshot and
collided; resolving the step lets each probe decide at its own arrival time, and the collisions
that cost time disappear. The same refinement restores the slingshot speed-up to its full value:
against powered flight the event-driven fold reproduces Nicholson and Forgan's roughly
two-orders-of-magnitude figure (about $166\times$ on a 400-star field), where the coarse step had
reported only about $20\times$. The celebrated speed-up survives contact with finite information;
the fill-time tax does not exist.

\subsection{The real cost is redundant travel, governed by $\Lambda = v/c$}
What the delay changes is how far the swarm flies. Because the field always fills and each
settlement launches a fixed number of offspring, both coordination regimes build essentially the
same number of probes -- differing by well under a percent, in a handful of terminal launches --
so the resource the delay moves is journeys, and stale views add wasted ones. That
redundant-travel tax is governed cleanly by the probe's speed in units of $c$. Table~\ref{tab:lambda}
and Fig.~\ref{fig:lambda} give it for powered flight swept across the speed axis: negligible at
the cruise speed, a median of 0.7\% of journeys at $\Lambda = 0.01$, rising monotonically to
18.4\% at $\Lambda = 0.2$. It is not marginal -- at every speed from $\Lambda = 0.03$ upward it is
positive in all 32 seeds ($p \approx 5\times10^{-10}$), and Fig.~\ref{fig:byseed} shows the
per-seed distributions sitting cleanly above zero. A small fill-time tax rides alongside it once
the swarm is fast, growing from nil at $\Lambda \le 0.01$ to a few percent at $\Lambda = 0.2$
(Table~\ref{tab:lambda}), but the redundant travel is the larger and cleaner signal, and unlike
the coarse-step time tax it is real: it does not vanish when the dynamics are resolved, because it
is not a batching artifact but the physical consequence of deciding on stale views.

This is what $\Lambda$ was always meant to govern. The ratio is the same on every hop, so it
cannot pick out where in the field the cost lands; but it sets, cleanly and monotonically, how
much of the swarm's motion is wasted. It is the right dimensionless group for the fuel tax, and it
does not predict a time tax because at resolved dynamics there is essentially none to predict.

\subsection{The fuel tax is a scale-stable fraction of effort}
The tax is not an artifact of the small field either. Fig.~\ref{fig:scale} sweeps the system size
at $\Lambda = 0.2$. The absolute count of wasted journeys grows with the field, as it must -- from
a median of about 230 extra trips at 300 stars to about 1{,}160 at 1{,}200 -- but as a fraction of
the perfect-information waste the tax is flat, 17.9\%, 18.9\%, 19.2\% at 300, 600, 1{,}200 stars,
positive in all 16 seeds at every size. The redundant-travel cost is thus a roughly
size-independent fraction of the swarm's effort, which is what makes the extrapolation toward
galactic scale a supported trend rather than a boundary effect of a small box.

\subsection{Where the cost falls}
Table~\ref{tab:regime} places the physical propulsion concepts on this axis. The source model's
powered cruise ($3\times10^{-5}\,c$) is far too slow for any race to matter, and pays nothing. The
gravitational slingshot that motivated this study reaches only $\Lambda \approx 0.01$ before its
boost self-limits, and pays about a percent -- real, but small. The tax becomes large only for
beamed directed-energy or light-sail propulsion at $0.1$ to $0.2\,c$
\cite{lubin-2016,carroll-nellenback-2019}, where it takes a fifth of journeys. Every case still
fills the field to completion: this is a property of the model, not a result, since with
re-targeting and no loss term the reachable field must close, and delay supplies no term that
would leave a permanent unsettled remainder of the kind an ``Aurora'' steady state requires
\cite{carroll-nellenback-2019}. The coordination failure shows up as wasted motion, not as an
unfilled galaxy.
