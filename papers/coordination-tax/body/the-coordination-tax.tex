\section{Results: the coordination tax}

We measured the cost as a paired ensemble: 32 seeded galaxies of 300 stars each, every one
filled twice, once with perfect information and once with the light-speed gate of
(\ref{eq:gate}), so that the only difference between the two runs of a pair is the delay.
The quantity of interest is the relative slowdown in the time to settle the entire field,
$t_{100}$, per seed. Reporting the distribution across the ensemble rather than a single
run follows the seeded Monte Carlo practice of the field \cite{forgan-2009}.

\begin{table}[!t]
  \centering
  \caption{Median fill-time penalty from light-speed lag, over the 32-seed paired
    ensemble at 300 stars. The penalty tracks hop locality, not raw speed.}
  \label{tab:penalty}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Flight policy & Median penalty (\%) & IQR (\%) \\
    \midrule
    Powered nearest-neighbour   & 0.0  & [0.0, 0.0]   \\
    Nearest-star slingshot      & 30.3 & [20.0, 38.5] \\
    Maximum-boost slingshot     & 51.4 & [46.0, 54.0] \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The medians and their spread are given in Table~\ref{tab:penalty} and shown per seed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:slowdown}. Powered flight pays essentially nothing; nearest-star slingshot
loses about a third of its time; maximum-boost slingshot loses about half. Two facts stand
out.

The first is that the penalty is \emph{not} simply $\Lambda = v/c$ from (\ref{eq:lambda}).
Powered flight is nearly untouched even though it is the slowest regime, and the two fast
slingshot regimes differ from each other by nearly a factor of two despite comparable
speeds. If $\Lambda$ alone set the cost, the fastest probes would pay the most in a smooth
progression; instead the cost tracks hop locality. A probe flying to the \emph{nearest}
star that loses a race has lost almost nothing: the next-nearest unsettled star is right
beside it, so the wasted trip and the recovery are both short. A probe chasing the
\emph{biggest boost} deliberately reaches past its neighbours to a distant star; when it
loses that race, both the wasted trip and the re-targeting detour are long. So
$\Lambda \approx v/c$ sets the \emph{scale} of the lag, but the \emph{non-locality of each
hop decides whether it bites}. Fig.~\ref{fig:settlement} shows the effect on a single
representative galaxy for the nearest-star policy: the light-speed run reaches full
settlement at about 110{,}000 years against 80{,}000 for the perfect-information run on the
same seed, a right-shift of roughly a third that is visible as a lag in the settlement
curve, not a change in its final height.

The second fact is what does \emph{not} happen: every case still fills the field to full
coverage. Light-speed lag makes the swarm slower and more wasteful, but it never leaves a
permanent unsettled remainder. Re-targeting guarantees that a probe which loses a race
simply picks another star, and eventually the field is closed. A permanent settled
fraction below one, a steady state in which some region stays forever empty, would require
settled sites to \emph{die}, adding a loss term to the dynamics; delay by itself supplies
no such term. Lag is a tax on speed, not a barrier to completion.
