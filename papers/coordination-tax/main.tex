\begin{abstract}
A swarm of self-replicating probes fills a stellar field star by star, each probe
settling a star and launching copies. The standard exploration-timescale model grants
every probe perfect, instantaneous knowledge of which stars are already settled, and
names finite light-speed as its own future work. We add that light-speed limit: a probe
deciding at a star treats a distant star as settled only once the news, travelling at
the speed of light, has arrived, so probes race for the same target from stale views and
waste trips. Over a 32-seed paired ensemble on identical galaxies, the delay slows the
time to fill the field by a median of about 0 percent for powered nearest-neighbour
flight, about 30 percent (95 percent bootstrap CI 24 to 38) for nearest-star slingshot
flight, and about 51 percent (47 to 54) for maximum-boost slingshot flight; the slingshot
penalties are positive in all 32 seeds, hold across coverage fractions from a quarter of
the field to all of it, and persist over a four-fold increase in field size. Every case
still reaches full coverage, so lag alone leaves no permanent unsettled fraction. The
ratio of information delay to travel time across a hop equals the probe speed in units of
the speed of light, and it gates whether the delay can bite at all: at the powered cruise
speed it is negligible. But because that ratio is the same on every hop, it does not
predict the penalty, which instead tracks how non-local each hop is: the two slingshot
policies reach comparable speeds, yet the one that reaches past its neighbours to distant
stars makes wasted trips about twice as long and pays about seventy percent more.
Fast-but-local flight is nearly immune; the cost falls on the long-range hops that made
the swarm fast. Under finite light-speed the slingshot speed-up is real, but a meaningful
fraction of it is eaten by uncoordinated long-range collisions.
\end{abstract}
