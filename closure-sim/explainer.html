Self-replicating factories in space

One factory makes two. Two make four. Then it hits a wall.

The long-term dream is to land a single robotic factory on the Moon and let it copy itself — two become four, four become eight — until you've grown an entire industry from one rocket's worth of cargo. Whether that ever works comes down to a single number, and one stubborn part the factory can't make.

FIG.1 — How a self-replicating factory grows, and where it gets stuck.

01  /  The idea — "closure"

How much of itself can it build?

Closure is the share of its own weight a factory can build from local material — the rock, metal and dust it digs up on site. Everything else has to keep arriving from Earth. The 1980 NASA study that first worked this out called those imported parts "vitamins": small, essential, and you can't survive without a steady supply.[1]

What it can make

Structural frames, solar mounts, motors, radiators — smelted and machined from local rock. Cheap to make, built on site.

What it can't

Computer chips, power electronics, precision sensors. The vitamins. Every one has to ride up from Earth.

Here's why this one number decides everything. Local parts grow with the factory — more machines make more parts, which is what powers the doubling. But vitamins arrive at a fixed trickle from Earth, no matter how big the factory gets. So the vitamins become the bottleneck: the factory can only grow as fast as its rarest essential part shows up.

NASA judged 90–96% closure achievable, and advised shipping the last 5–10% as vitamins rather than chasing perfection.[1] The deeper rule: below full self-sufficiency a factory stays tethered to resupply; only as it nears 100% can growth truly run away.[2] More recent work puts realistic near-term closure around 70% — with chips, solar cells and circuitry still coming from home.[3]

02  /  The wall — why chips are the hard part

A chip is the tip of the deepest supply chain on Earth.

~600× The fuel, water, chemicals and gases needed to make one 2-gram microchip outweigh the chip itself about 600 times.[4]

Making a chip takes 400-plus separate manufacturing steps and materials purified to 99.9999999%.[7] You cannot recreate that on the Moon with a handful of robots. And the energy alone is staggering: a finished chip takes roughly 3,000–15,000 kilowatt-hours of electricity per kilogram to manufacture.[4][5] That's about a thousand times the energy the same factory spends to smelt a kilogram of metal from local rock.*

* Our model uses 8,000 kWh/kg for chips against ~5–8 kWh/kg for smelted metal — both inside the cited ranges. Even making solar cells locally is its own energy sink: purifying silicon runs 40–120 kWh/kg.[6]

03  /  The double bind

So why not just make the chips on site?

Because that swaps one wall for another. The factory is trapped from both directions:

Ship the chips in

The factory grows fast at first, then slows to the speed of the rocket schedule. It can only build as quickly as its rarest part arrives — so it crawls.

Make the chips itself

Now it's limited by electricity. Chips are so power-hungry the factory runs out of energy before it runs out of parts. Closing the gap would need a power plant heavier than the factory — and today's best lunar reactor design makes just 40 kW at under 6 tonnes.[8]

Chips are a "vitamin" for a reason. A factory only beats the wall if it's already highly self-sufficient and swimming in power — both at once.

04  /  What the model shows

Years saved, or never at all.

Running a sample lunar seed factory through the trade-off above — goal: reach 1,000 kg of output per day — the choices play out like this:

Lunar seed factory · time to reach the goal
Chips shipped from Earth~29 years
Chips made on site, with a 4 MW power plant~17 years
Chips made on site, with only 1 MWnever — runs out of power
